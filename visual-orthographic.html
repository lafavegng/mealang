Visual & Orthographic Psychology

MEA Negative-Space Logo: M as mountain of potential; hover reveals E (energy bridge) and A (alliance peak).

The Inner Loop: From Sight to Sound. The phonological loop is a component of your working memory that acts like an 'inner ear' and an 'inner voice'. In reading, its primary job is to translate visual symbols (letters) into sound-based codes. This allows you to 'hear' the words in your head, holding them in memory just long enough to stitch them together into meaningful sentences and complex ideas.

Inner Loop Diagram

1. Visual Encoding

When your eyes scan a word, the 'Visuo-spatial Sketchpad' captures the image. However, to understand language, the brain must quickly convert these shapes into a phonological (sound-based) representation.

2. The Phonological Store

Also known as the 'Inner Ear', this temporary buffer holds the sound-traces for about 1.5 to 2 seconds. Without further action, these sounds will rapidly decay and be forgotten.

3. Articulatory Rehearsal

To keep the sounds alive, the 'Inner Voice' (Articulatory Rehearsal) repeats the words sub-vocally. This 'loop' refreshes the information, allowing you to finish a long sentence without forgetting how it started.

4. Semantic Integration

Once the full sentence structure is held in the loop, the Central Executive retrieves meanings from long-term memory, allowing you to grasp the overall context and intent of the text.

Orthographic Mapping

Our brains utilize Orthographic Mapping to store word forms. When a word like 'MEA' is all-caps, it loses the 'ascender' and 'descender' clues that allow for rapid sub-conscious recognition, forcing the brain to process it as a Logograph (a whole-word symbol). Conversely, lowercase 'mea' fits seamlessly into the eye's natural saccades (skips) across a line of text, making it feel 'softer' and more integrated into a narrative flow.

All-Caps

Creates a uniform rectangular block. This requires more cognitive effort to decode because the distinctive 'outline' of the word is lost.

Lowercase

The fluid, varying heights of the letters provide 'landmarks' for the eye, making the reading experience feel effortless and natural.

Visual Saliency/Hierarchy Principles

Visual hierarchy isn't just about what you add; it's about the space you leave behind. Spatial arrangement defines the relationship between elements.

  • White Space (Negative Space): Provides 'breathing room' and isolates focal points. It increases comprehension by up to 20%.
  • Proximity: Placing items close together suggests they are related (e.g., an image and its caption).
  • Alignment: Creates a visual connection between elements, giving the design balance and an orderly flow.

Scanning Patterns

F-Pattern

Common for text-heavy pages like blogs. Users read horizontally across the top, then vertically down the left side.

Best For: Articles, Blogs

Key Real Estate: Top bar, Left margin

Behavior: Skimming for keywords

Z-Pattern

Ideal for visual-heavy pages or landing pages. The eye moves in a 'Z' shape from top-left to bottom-right.

Best For: Landing Pages

Key Real Estate: Hero banner, Call to Action

Behavior: Quick scanning for value
